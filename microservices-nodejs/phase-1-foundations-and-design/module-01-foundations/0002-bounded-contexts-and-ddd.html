Bounded Contexts & DDD

How to draw service boundaries that match the business — not your folder structure. The framework that tells you where the lines go, and why your 30-module monolith isn't 30 microservices.

⏱ 30 min read Phase 1 — Foundations & Design Prereq: Lesson 1

📖 Primary source: Martin Fowler, "Bounded Context" — the clearest explanation of the concept. Read alongside Sam Newman, Building Microservices Ch. 2 ("How to Model Services") and Eric Evans, Domain-Driven Design Ch. 14 ("Maintaining Model Integrity").

Your folder structure is not a service map

You told me your monolith has ~30 modules under service/v1/ — campaign, influencer, brand, finance, analytics, notification, coupon, package, and more. Your instinct is to say: "Each of these could be a microservice."

This is the #1 decomposition mistake

Mapping folders to services is technical decomposition — slicing by code structure, not by business capability.[1] The result: 30 services that constantly call each other, share database tables, and can't be deployed independently. You've just built a distributed monolith with 30x the operational cost.

The right question isn't "which folders can I extract?" It's: "Which parts of the business can make decisions without asking anyone else?"

That question is what Domain-Driven Design answers.

What DDD actually is (and isn't)

Eric Evans introduced Domain-Driven Design in 2003.[2] It's a 560-page book, and 90% of the internet gets it wrong. Here's what matters for microservice boundaries:

DDD has two halves — you only need one right now

Strategic Design (the half we need): how to divide a large system into Bounded Contexts and manage the relationships between them. This tells you where the service boundaries go.
Tactical Design (the half we don't need yet): Entities, Value Objects, Aggregates, Repositories — how to structure code inside a single service. We'll cover this in Module 02.

DDD is not: an architecture pattern, a coding framework, or something that requires special libraries. It's a way of thinking about where business boundaries are. You can apply Strategic DDD to your existing monolith right now, without changing a single line of code.

Ubiquitous Language — the boundary detector

The most powerful concept in DDD is also the simplest: Ubiquitous Language.

Within a single team or context, every term should mean exactly one thing.[2] Developers, product managers, and domain experts should all use the same words — and those words should appear in the code.

The test — applied to your codebase

Think about the word "campaign" in your system. Does it mean the same thing everywhere?

  • In service/v1/campaign/: a brand's marketing campaign with deliverables, timelines, and creator assignments
  • In service/v1/music-campaign/: a music promotion with different rules, different metrics, different lifecycle
  • In service/v1/analytics/: a data record to aggregate performance metrics against
  • In service/v1/finance/: a billing entity that triggers payments to creators

When the same word means different things in different parts of the system, you've found a context boundary.[3] A "campaign" in Finance is not the same model as a "campaign" in Analytics. They have different fields, different rules, and different reasons to change. Forcing them into a single Campaign model is what creates the coupling you're fighting.

Bounded Context — the actual boundary

A Bounded Context is the boundary within which a particular domain model is defined and consistent.[3] Inside a Bounded Context:

  • Every term has one meaning (the Ubiquitous Language holds)
  • The model is internally consistent — no contradictions
  • The team can change the model without coordinating with other contexts
The critical insight

A Bounded Context is not the same thing as a microservice.[3] A Bounded Context is a conceptual boundary. A microservice is a deployment boundary. They often align, but the mapping is not always 1:1.

  • One Bounded Context → one microservice (common, clean)
  • One Bounded Context → multiple microservices (when the context is large enough to warrant it)
  • Multiple Bounded Contexts → one deployment (the Modular Monolith — still valid!)

How to find your Bounded Contexts

Fowler's advice:[3] don't try to build one unified model for the whole system. Instead, identify the natural boundaries where language diverges. Three heuristics:

Heuristic How to apply it Example from your codebase
Language divergence Where the same word means different things "Campaign" in campaign/ vs finance/ vs analytics/
Rate of change Which parts change together vs independently Auth rarely changes; Campaign changes weekly
Team ownership Who "owns" this business capability? Does the same team own both influencer/ and talentAgency/?

Your 30 modules → maybe 6 Bounded Contexts

Let's walk through your service/v1/ directory and group modules into candidate Bounded Contexts. This is a first draft — the real discovery happens by talking to domain experts and watching where language diverges.

Candidate Bounded Context Modules that likely belong Why they're cohesive
Campaign Management campaign, music-campaign Both are variants of "a brand hires creators to produce content." Same business capability, same lifecycle patterns, different configurations.
Creator (Influencer) Identity influencer, talentAgency, xCommunity Who creators are, their profiles, their relationships with agencies. The "creator" model here is about identity and representation.
Finance & Payments finance, cashfreeKyc, coupon, earnkaro Money flowing: payouts, KYC verification, coupons, affiliate earnings. All need transaction guarantees.
Brand & Commerce brand, package, serviceRequests The buyer side: brand onboarding, service packages, brand-initiated requests.
Communication & Engagement notification, mailTrigger, whatsappTrigger All about reaching users through different channels. The business concept is "send a message" — the channel is an implementation detail.
Intelligence & Data analytics, report, soundCharts, xley-ai, openai Read-heavy, analytics-focused. Different data models, different query patterns, different scaling needs.
That leaves auth, permission, healthChecker, redisData, shortLink, tenant, trulioo, creatorsUnited, masterclassVideo as either supporting subdomains or cross-cutting concerns — not Bounded Contexts of their own.

Notice: 30 modules → 6 candidate contexts. That's the power of thinking in business capabilities instead of code structure. Six services is manageable. Thirty is a maintenance nightmare.

Not all contexts are equal — Core vs Supporting vs Generic

Evans introduces a concept called distillation:[2] not all parts of your system deserve equal investment. There are three types of subdomains:

Type Definition In your system Build or buy?
Core Domain The thing that makes your business unique. What you'd pitch to investors. Your competitive advantage. Campaign Management + Creator Matching — this is what Xley does Build (invest deeply)
Supporting Necessary for the Core to function, but not your unique value proposition. Custom, but not strategic. Brand onboarding, Analytics dashboards, Creator profiles Build (keep lean)
Generic Solved problems. Not specific to your business. Everyone needs this. Auth, Notifications, KYC, Payments processing, Short links Buy / use SaaS / open-source
The investment trap

Most engineering teams over-invest in Generic subdomains (building custom auth systems, custom notification pipelines) and under-invest in the Core Domain. Every hour spent perfecting your notification system is an hour not spent on what makes your business different. This is why microservice boundaries matter — they force you to make this prioritisation explicit.[1]

Context Mapping — how contexts relate to each other

Once you've identified your Bounded Contexts, the next question is: how do they communicate?[2] In your monolith, the answer is "direct function calls and shared database tables." In a microservices world, you need explicit relationships. DDD defines several patterns:

Pattern What it means When you'd use it In your system
Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL) A translation layer that converts another context's model into your own When integrating with a legacy or external system whose model you don't control Your cashfreeKyc module wrapping Cashfree's API — you're already doing this
Customer–Supplier Upstream context serves downstream; downstream can influence the upstream's priorities When one team depends on another and they can negotiate Campaign Management (customer) ← Creator Identity (supplier): campaigns need creator data
Conformist Downstream accepts the upstream's model as-is, no translation When you have no leverage over the upstream (e.g., third-party APIs) Your soundCharts module conforming to SoundCharts' API model exactly
Shared Kernel Two contexts share a small, explicit subset of the model When duplication is truly harmful and teams coordinate tightly Your shared helper.js (3600+ lines) is an accidental shared kernel
Partnership Two contexts evolve together, coordinating releases When two features must succeed or fail together Campaign + Finance (payout happens when campaign completes)
Your helper.js is a coupling bomb

A 3,600-line shared utility file that every module imports is the definition of an accidental Shared Kernel. In DDD terms, it means every module is implicitly coupled to every other module that uses the same helper function. A change to a date formatting function in helper.js can break campaign creation, analytics reports, and payment processing simultaneously.

In a microservices world, this file must be either:

  • Split — each context gets only the utilities it needs (copied, not shared)
  • Published — turned into a versioned internal npm package with semver guarantees
  • Eliminated — most "utility" functions belong inside the context that uses them

The Anti-Corruption Layer — your most important pattern

When two Bounded Contexts communicate, the worst thing that can happen is model leakage: one context's internal model bleeds into another, coupling them at the data-structure level.[2]

The Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL) prevents this by acting as a translator. Here's what it looks like in your monolith today vs. what it should look like:

❌ What you probably have now

// campaign/campaign-helper.js
const influencerService = require('../influencer/influencer');

async function assignCreatorToCampaign(campaignId, influencerId) {
  // Direct coupling: Campaign knows Influencer's internal model
  const influencer = await influencerService.getInfluencer(influencerId);

  // Campaign code reaches into Influencer's internal fields
  const rate = influencer.pricing.perPostRate;
  const platform = influencer.socialAccounts[0].platform;
  const followers = influencer.socialAccounts[0].followers;
  // ...
}

✅ What an ACL looks like

// campaign/acl/creator-adapter.js
// Translates the Creator context's model into Campaign's language

class CreatorAdapter {
  constructor(creatorService) {
    this.creatorService = creatorService;
  }

  async getAssignableCreator(creatorId) {
    const raw = await this.creatorService.getInfluencer(creatorId);

    // Translation: Campaign context only knows what it needs
    return {
      id:           raw.id,
      displayName:  raw.name,
      rate:         raw.pricing?.perPostRate ?? null,
      reach:        raw.socialAccounts?.[0]?.followers ?? 0,
      platform:     raw.socialAccounts?.[0]?.platform ?? 'unknown',
    };
  }
}

The key insight: Campaign doesn't know (or care) that the creator data has socialAccounts[0].followers. It only knows about reach. When the Creator context changes its internal model, only the adapter breaks — not the entire Campaign service.

Why "where do I draw the lines?" was the wrong question

You came into this course asking: "Where do I draw the service boundaries in my monolith?" After this lesson, you should see why that question was premature.

The right sequence is:

  1. Find the language boundaries — where does the same word mean different things?
  2. Identify the Bounded Contexts — group modules by business capability, not code structure
  3. Classify them — Core, Supporting, or Generic?
  4. Map the relationships — which Context Mapping pattern fits each relationship?
  5. Only then — decide which contexts deserve to be independently deployable services

Step 5 is where microservices enter the picture. Steps 1–4 are pure DDD, and they're valuable even if you never extract a single service. A Modular Monolith with well-defined Bounded Contexts is a perfectly valid architecture.

1. What is a Bounded Context, and how does it differ from a microservice? RECALL-1D
Reveal answer

A Bounded Context is a conceptual boundary within which a domain model and its language are consistent. A microservice is a deployment boundary. They often align, but a context can contain multiple services, or multiple contexts can live in one deployment (Modular Monolith).

2. What is Ubiquitous Language, and how do you detect that it has broken down? RECALL-1D
Reveal answer

Ubiquitous Language is a shared, precise vocabulary used consistently by developers and domain experts within a Bounded Context. It breaks down when the same word means different things in different parts of the system — that divergence signals a context boundary.

3. Name the three types of subdomains in DDD and give an example from an influencer marketing platform. RECALL-1D
Reveal answer

Core Domain — Campaign Management + Creator Matching (competitive advantage).
Supporting Subdomain — Analytics dashboards, Brand onboarding (necessary but not unique).
Generic Subdomain — Auth, Notifications, KYC (solved problems; buy or reuse).

4. What is an Anti-Corruption Layer, and why is it critical when two contexts communicate? RECALL-3D
Reveal answer

An ACL is a translation layer that converts one context's model into another's language. Without it, one context's internal model leaks into another, coupling them at the data-structure level. When the upstream context changes its model, the downstream context breaks — unless the ACL absorbs the change.

5. Why is mapping folders to services considered the #1 decomposition mistake? RECALL-3D
Reveal answer

Folders represent technical structure (code organisation), not business capability. Services split by folder produce a distributed monolith: many services that share databases, call each other for every operation, and can't deploy independently. You get all the operational complexity with none of the autonomy.

6. What are the five DDD Context Mapping patterns, and which one describes your helper.js? RECALL-3D
Reveal answer

Partnership, Shared Kernel, Customer–Supplier, Conformist, Anti-Corruption Layer.
helper.js is an accidental Shared Kernel: a large, unversioned body of shared code that couples every module that imports it. Changes propagate unpredictably.

7. You have 30 service modules. After applying DDD Strategic Design, roughly how many Bounded Contexts did the analysis suggest, and why the dramatic reduction? RECALL-7D
Reveal answer

~6 Bounded Contexts. The reduction comes from grouping by business capability instead of code structure. Many modules (e.g., notification, mailTrigger, whatsappTrigger) serve the same business function through different channels — they're one context, not three services.

8. What is the correct sequence of steps before deciding which contexts become microservices? RECALL-7D
Reveal answer

1. Find language boundaries. 2. Identify Bounded Contexts. 3. Classify as Core/Supporting/Generic. 4. Map relationships (Context Mapping). 5. Only then decide which contexts merit independent deployment. Steps 1–4 are valuable even in a monolith.
