What Microservices Actually Are

Why your monolith is not "wrong," what problems microservices actually solve, and how to recognise when you have those problems.

⏱ 25 min read Phase 1 — Foundations & Design Prereq: Your Express monolith mental model

📖 Primary source: Martin Fowler's Microservices Guide — the field's canonical definition, characteristics, and the "Microservice Premium" concept. Read alongside Sam Newman, Building Microservices Ch. 1.

The problem you already have

You said your Express monolith has clear module separation — routes, controllers, services, models. That's good architecture. But here's the problem it doesn't solve:

The deployment coupling problem

When the Orders team merges a change, the entire application redeploys. The Users team can't ship without waiting for Orders. The Payments team can't deploy a hotfix without a full release cycle. Everyone is coupled not by code, but by the deployment unit.

Your folder structure inside the monolith is like having separate departments on different floors of the same office building. They're logically separated, but they all share the same elevator — the elevator being your single node server.js process. If the elevator breaks (a crash), everyone is stuck. If one team needs to renovate their floor, the whole building goes offline.

Microservices replace the one elevator with many elevators. Each team gets their own building. They can renovate, expand, or shut down independently. But now you have to manage many buildings, coordinate when someone needs to cross the street, and handle what happens when the bridge between buildings closes.

That last part — the coordination cost — is what the microservices industry spent a decade learning to manage. This course is the compressed version of those lessons.

Formal definition & what it actually means

Martin Fowler's definition[1] (the one the field uses): microservices are an architectural style where a single application is developed as a suite of small, independently deployable services, each running in its own process, communicating over lightweight mechanisms, and deployed independently.

The three words that matter most are: independently deployable. Not "small" — that's a symptom, not the goal. Not "single responsibility" — that's a design principle, not a boundary condition. The true test of a microservice is: can it be deployed without any other service being redeployed?

The criminally underrated option

The Modular Monolith gives you most of the code-organisation benefits of microservices with almost none of the operational cost. If your team is small (<10 engineers), you have one deployment pipeline, or you're early in a product's life — a Modular Monolith is almost certainly the right call. Amazon, Netflix, and Uber all started as monoliths.[2]

Conway's Law — the hidden force shaping your architecture

Melvin Conway (1968)[3]: "Any organisation that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organisation's communication structure."

In plain terms: your team structure becomes your system architecture, whether you plan it or not. If your company has three teams — Frontend, Backend, Database — you will build a three-tier architecture. Not because it's the best design, but because that's how your humans communicate.

The Inverse Conway Maneuver

Amazon's approach (Bezos' "two-pizza team" mandate)[4]: deliberately restructure the organisation first to match the desired architecture, then let the architecture emerge. If you want Orders and Users to be independently deployable, you need an Orders team and a Users team who can ship without talking to each other. Without that, your "microservices" are still coupled at the team-coordination layer — which is worse than a monolith.

Conway in action
1 Backend Team → produces → 1 Monolithic API
Everyone coordinates every deployment. Fast at first, painful at scale.
Orders Team Users Team Payments Team → produces → 3 Independent Services
Each team ships independently. Coordination happens through APIs, not Slack.

How real companies actually did this

Amazon — the mandate that changed the industry

In 2002, Jeff Bezos issued a memo[4] requiring every team to expose data and functionality only through service APIs — no exceptions, no back-channel access, no shared databases. This wasn't an engineering decision; it was an organisational one. The result: Amazon's internal services became the foundation of AWS. EC2, S3, and SQS were built for internal use first. Conway's Law, deliberately applied.

Netflix — from DVD-by-mail to 700+ services

Netflix's 2008 database corruption incident took their entire service offline for three days[5] — one shared database, one point of failure. They migrated to AWS and decomposed into microservices over seven years. By 2016: 700+ services, ~5,000 production changes per day. What they learned the hard way: distributed failures are non-deterministic, so they invented chaos engineering — deliberately injecting failures in production. Netflix Hystrix (circuit breaker library) came directly from these war stories.

Uber — the distributed monolith trap

Uber split into 2,000+ microservices by 2018.[6] Then they discovered the problem: too many services created a new kind of tight coupling. A change in one service cascaded through 40 dependent services. They'd replaced compile-time coupling with runtime coupling — and runtime coupling is harder to detect. Uber responded with Domain-Oriented Microservice Architecture (DOMA): grouping services into domains with clear ownership, reducing the blast radius of any single change.

Spotify — the squad model

Spotify built their architecture around autonomous "squads"[7] — small cross-functional teams that own a feature end-to-end (frontend, backend, data, ops). Each squad owns their service(s) completely, from code to production. This is the organisational structure that makes microservices actually work. Without it, you get the coordination overhead of microservices without the autonomy benefits.

When microservices are the wrong answer

The Microservice Premium — Martin Fowler

The upfront cost you pay with microservices[8]: distributed systems complexity, network latency, operational overhead, debugging difficulty. You only come out ahead when the productivity benefits of team independence outweigh that upfront cost. Most teams never reach the scale where this crossover happens.

Do not use microservices if:

  • Your team is fewer than 10–15 engineers (coordination overhead exceeds autonomy gains)
  • You haven't built the product yet — you don't know where the domain boundaries are
  • You lack strong DevOps / SRE capability (microservices multiply operational complexity)
  • Your services would share a database anyway — that's a distributed monolith, not microservices
  • You can't articulate which team owns which service — no org alignment means no benefit
The "Monolith First" argument

Sam Newman (Building Microservices)[9] and Martin Fowler[2] both argue: build the monolith first. You can only draw correct service boundaries after you understand the domain well. Premature decomposition is one of the most common and costly distributed systems mistakes.

Common mistakes — especially from a monolith background

Mistake Why monolith engineers make it The fix
Decompose by technical layer Your monolith has routes/controllers/services/models, so you split the same way — producing a "Data Service" and "API Service" that both hit the same DB Decompose by business capability, not technical layer (Module 02)
Shared database Easiest way to share data — it's what you do in the monolith Database-per-service. Each service owns its data (Module 04)
Synchronous everything Every function call in Express returns immediately — you expect the same from services Learn when to use async messaging instead (Module 03)
Too many services too fast More services = more "micro" = more correct (wrong) "Death by a thousand services" anti-pattern — Module 02
No API contract discipline In the monolith, the compiler catches breaking changes. In microservices, Service B crashes silently at 2am when Service A changes its response shape API versioning and consumer-driven contract testing
Exercise — Module 01, Lesson 1

Map your monolith's coupling surface

Take your actual Express monolith (or your closest mental model of one). Draw — on paper or in Excalidraw — an architecture diagram with the following:

  1. List your 4–6 main functional areas (e.g. Users, Orders, Products, Payments, Notifications, Admin). These are your potential service candidates.
  2. Draw lines between them for every place where one area reads or writes another area's data directly — via a shared function call or a shared DB table.
  3. Circle any area where changes are most frequently blocked by another area's release.
  4. Write one sentence for each candidate: "If this was its own service, which team would own it, and what would they need from other services?"

Success criterion: You can identify your top 2 candidates for service extraction and explain what problem that extraction solves — not just that they could be separate.

🧠 Retrieval Practice

Answer from memory before revealing. The slight difficulty is the point — it's where storage strength builds.

1. What is the true test of a microservice — not "small" or "single responsibility," but the one property that defines it? RECALL-1D
Reveal answer

Independent deployability: can it be deployed without any other service being redeployed? Size and responsibility are design guidance. This is the architectural boundary condition.

2. State Conway's Law and give a concrete example from a real company. RECALL-1D
Reveal answer

"Any organisation that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organisation's communication structure." Example: Amazon restructured into two-pizza teams before building service APIs — the org change enabled the architectural change, not the other way around.

3. What is a distributed monolith, and why is it worse than an actual monolith? RECALL-1D
Reveal answer

A distributed monolith is a microservices deployment where services are still tightly coupled — typically through a shared database or deep synchronous call chains. It's worse because you have all the operational complexity of distributed systems (network failures, partial failures, tracing) without any of the autonomy benefits.

4. What is the "Microservice Premium" and when, if ever, does it pay off? RECALL-3D
Reveal answer

The upfront cost of microservices complexity. It pays off only when the productivity gains from team independence — independent deployability and scaling — outweigh the distributed systems overhead. Most teams never reach this crossover. Fowler argues most should start with a monolith.

5. Name three concrete signs that a monolith should not be split into microservices yet. RECALL-3D
Reveal answer

1. Team is fewer than ~10 engineers (overhead exceeds autonomy gain). 2. The product domain isn't understood well enough to draw correct boundaries. 3. No strong DevOps/SRE capability to manage N services in production.

6. What mistake did Uber make going to 2,000+ services, and how did they recover? RECALL-7D
Reveal answer

Changes in one service cascaded through 40 dependent services — runtime coupling replaced compile-time coupling, which is harder to detect. Recovery: Domain-Oriented Microservice Architecture (DOMA) — grouping services into domains with clear ownership, reducing blast radius.

7. The Modular Monolith vs Microservices: what is the one critical difference in how they behave at runtime? RECALL-3D
Reveal answer

In a Modular Monolith, all calls between modules are in-process function calls: they cannot fail due to network issues, they have no latency, and they don't need retry logic. In microservices, every call crosses the network: it can fail, timeout, or return partial data. This single difference drives most of the distributed systems complexity.

8. What was the Bezos API Mandate, and what architectural outcome did it enable? RECALL-7D
Reveal answer

A 2002 internal directive requiring every Amazon team to expose data/functionality only via service APIs — no direct function calls, no shared databases. Non-compliance meant firing. It enabled AWS: the internal services built to serve Amazon.com (compute, storage, queuing) became products sold externally. Organisational mandate → architectural outcome. (Inverse Conway Maneuver)

Interview preparation

Tier 1 — Startup / Mid-Market
  • What is a microservice? How is it different from a module in a monolith?
  • When would you choose a monolith over microservices?
  • What does "independently deployable" mean in practice?
  • Have you seen Conway's Law in action in a real codebase?
Tier 2 — Senior Engineer (FAANG-Adjacent)
  • Explain the Microservice Premium. Describe a situation where microservices are actively the wrong choice.
  • What is a distributed monolith? How does it happen and how do you detect it before it's too late?
  • Your team wants to split a 200k-line Express monolith into microservices. What is your very first step?
  • How does Conway's Law affect how you'd structure teams around a proposed service architecture?
Tier 3 — Staff Engineer (FAANG)
  • How do you evaluate whether a proposed service boundary is correct before you commit to building it?
  • Your org is moving to microservices but teams have not reorganised. Predict what will go wrong and how you'd address it architecturally.
  • Describe how Uber's DOMA approach addresses the "too many services" problem and what tradeoffs it introduces.
  • At what point in a company's evolution do microservices pay off? How would you measure that inflection point?
Tier 4 — Production Scenario
  • It's 2am. Service A is returning 503s. Monitoring shows it calls Service B → Service C → shared DB. Walk me through your diagnosis and what architectural changes you'd propose to prevent recurrence.
  • You've joined a team with 50 microservices and 5 engineers. Two engineers left last month. Nobody knows which service owns user session data. What do you do first?
