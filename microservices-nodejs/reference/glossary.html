Glossary

Canonical terminology for Microservices & Distributed Systems. Terms are added after the learner has encountered them in a lesson.

Architecture Styles
Monolith

A single deployment unit containing all application functionality. One process, one codebase, one release cycle. All function calls are in-process — they cannot fail due to network issues.

Avoid: using "monolith" as a pejorative. A well-structured monolith is a legitimate architectural choice.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Modular Monolith

A monolith with enforced internal module boundaries (separate packages, well-defined APIs between modules, no cross-module database access). Gives most code-organisation benefits of microservices with almost none of the operational complexity. Still a single deployment unit.

Avoid: conflating with microservices — the key difference is that all calls remain in-process.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Microservice

An independently deployable service that runs in its own process, owns its own data, and communicates with other services over the network. The defining property is independent deployability — not size, not "single responsibility."

Avoid: defining by size ("small service"). The boundary condition is deployability, not lines of code.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Distributed Monolith

A microservices deployment where services are still tightly coupled — through a shared database, deep synchronous call chains, or coordinated releases. Worse than an actual monolith: you get all the operational complexity of distributed systems without any of the autonomy benefits.

Avoid: "loosely coupled microservices" when describing systems with shared databases.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Design Principles
Independent Deployability

The ability to deploy a service without requiring any other service to be redeployed simultaneously. This is the true test of a microservice — the architectural boundary condition from which all other properties flow.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Conway's Law

"Any organisation that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organisation's communication structure." — Melvin Conway, 1968. In practice: your team structure becomes your system architecture, whether you plan it or not.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Inverse Conway Maneuver

Deliberately restructuring the organisation first to match the desired system architecture, then letting the architecture emerge naturally. Example: Amazon's two-pizza team mandate restructured the org before building service APIs.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Microservice Premium

The upfront cost you pay with microservices: distributed systems complexity, network latency, operational overhead, debugging difficulty. You only come out ahead when the productivity benefits of team independence outweigh this cost. Most teams never reach the scale where this crossover happens. — Martin Fowler

Introduced: Lesson 01

Organisational Patterns
DOMA (Domain-Oriented Microservice Architecture)

Uber's approach to managing thousands of microservices: grouping services into domains with clear ownership, reducing the blast radius of any single change. Addresses the "too many services" problem by adding an organisational layer above individual services.

Introduced: Lesson 01

Two-Pizza Team

Amazon's rule of thumb: a team should be small enough to be fed by two pizzas (~6–8 people). Part of the Inverse Conway Maneuver — small autonomous teams naturally produce small autonomous services.

Introduced: Lesson 01
